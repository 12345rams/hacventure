What are the types of cases in police?
There are different types of cases in police depending on the nature and severity of the offence, the procedure followed by the police, and the jurisdiction of the court. Some of the common types of cases in police are:

Cognizable cases: These are cases where the police can arrest the accused without a warrant and start an investigation without the permission of a magistrate. These are usually serious offences such as murder, rape, robbery, etc12 Non-cognizable cases: These are cases where the police cannot arrest the accused without a warrant or start an investigation without the order of a magistrate. These are usually minor offences such as trespassing, cheating, defamation, etc12
Bailable cases: These are cases where the accused has a right to get bail from the police or the court. Bail is a security given by the accused to ensure his or her appearance before the court. The amount and conditions of bail depend on various factors such as the nature of the offence, the character of the accused, etc12 Non-bailable cases: These are cases where the accused does not have a right to get bail from the police or the court. Bail can only be granted by the court at its discretion after considering various factors such as the gravity of the offence, the risk of tampering with evidence or witnesses, etc12 Summary cases: These are cases where the trial is conducted by a magistrate without a jury or witnesses. These are usually minor offences such as causing a disturbance, petty theft, etc. The maximum punishment for these offences is six months imprisonment or a fine of Rs 2000 or both13 Indictable cases: These are cases where the trial is conducted by a judge with or without a jury and with witnesses. These are usually serious offences such as theft, break and enter, murder, etc. The punishment for these offences can range from imprisonment for a term to death penalty in some cases13 These are some of the types of cases in police that are commonly encountered in India and other countries. Each type of case has different legal implications and consequences for the accused and the victim.

What are the rights of a person arrested by the police?
person arrested by the police has certain rights under the law that are meant to protect his or her dignity, liberty and human rights. Some of the rights of a person arrested by the police are:

Right to know the grounds of arrest: The police officer or any other person arresting a person without a warrant must inform the arrested person about the offence and the grounds for such arrest12 This right is also guaranteed by Article 22(1) of the Constitution of India3Right to be produced before a magistrate without unnecessary delay: The police officer making an arrest without a warrant must produce the arrested person before a magistrate having jurisdiction or an officer in charge of a police station within 24 hours of the arrest, excluding the time taken for travel14 This right is also guaranteed by Article 22(2) of the Constitution of India3 Right to be released on bail: The arrested person has a right to be released on bail if the offence for which he or she is arrested is bailable. Bail is a security given by the arrested person or someone on his or her behalf to ensure his or her appearance before the court. The amount and conditions of bail depend on various factors such as the nature and gravity of the offence, the character and antecedents of the accused, etc12
Right to a fair trial: The arrested person has a right to a fair trial by an impartial and competent court in accordance with the law. The trial must be conducted in an open court and in the presence of the accused. The accused has a right to be defended by a lawyer of his or her choice or by a legal aid lawyer if he or she cannot afford one. The accused also has a right to examine the witnesses and evidence against him or her and to produce his or her own witnesses and evidence in his or her defence. The accused also has a right to appeal against the conviction or sentence by a higher court123 Right to consult a lawyer: The arrested person has a right to consult a lawyer of his or her choice during interrogation, investigation and trial. The lawyer can advise the arrested person about his or her rights and obligations under the law and can represent him or her before the court. The police officer must inform the arrested person of this right and allow him or her reasonable time and facilities to consult a lawyer12 Right to free legal aid: The arrested person has a right to free legal aid if he or she cannot afford a lawyer. Free legal aid means providing legal services such as advice, representation, drafting of documents, etc., free of cost to eligible persons by lawyers appointed by the state or by voluntary organisations. The police officer must inform the arrested person of this right and provide him or her with necessary information and assistance to avail free legal aid125
Right to remain silent: The arrested person has a right to remain silent during interrogation, investigation and trial. He or she cannot be compelled to answer any question or make any statement that may incriminate him or her. He or she can also refuse to sign any document that may be used against him or her as evidence. This right is based on the principle that no one can be forced to be a witness against himself or herself. This right is also guaranteed by Article 20(3) of the Constitution of India123 Right to be examined by a medical practitioner: The arrested person has a right to be examined by a medical practitioner at the time of arrest and at any time during custody. The medical examination is done to ascertain the physical and mental condition of the arrested person and to detect any injuries, illnesses or signs of torture or ill-treatment. The medical report must be recorded in writing and given to the arrested person and his or her lawyer. The medical examination must be done by an independent and qualified doctor in accordance with professional ethics and standards12

What are the duties of the police during arrest?
The police have certain duties and responsibilities during arrest that are meant to ensure the lawfulness, fairness and transparency of the arrest process. Some of the duties of the police during arrest are:

To inform the arrested person about the offence and the grounds for such arrest12 This duty is based on Section 50 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) and Article 22(1) of the Constitution of India3 To produce the arrested person before a magistrate within 24 hours of the arrest, excluding the time taken for travel14 This duty is based on Section 57 of the CrPC and Article 22(2) of the Constitution of India3 To inform the arrested person of his or her right to consult a lawyer and to provide him or her reasonable time and facilities to do so12 This duty is based on Section 41D of the CrPC and Article 22(1) of the Constitution of India3 To inform the arrested person of his or her right to free legal aid if he or she cannot afford a lawyer and to provide him or her with necessary information and assistance to avail it125 This duty is based on Section 304 of the CrPC and Article 39A of the Constitution of India3
To inform a nominated person of the arrested person’s choice about his or her arrest and place of detention as soon as possible12 This duty is based on Section 50A of the CrPC. To examine the arrested person by a medical practitioner at the time of arrest and at any time during custody and to record and provide a copy of the medical report to the arrested person and his or her lawyer12 This duty is based on Section 54 of the CrPC. To treat the arrested person with dignity and respect and to refrain from any torture, ill-treatment or coercion that may violate his or her human rights12 This duty is based on various national and international laws and conventions such as the Indian Evidence Act, 1872, the Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993, the Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment, 1984, etc.

What is bail?
Bail is the conditional release of a person accused of a crime, for an amount, pledged for the appearance of the accused when the time comes.

The person paying the money acts as the surety12. Bail is not a free pass out of the criminal trial, but a way to ensure that the accused does not suffer unnecessary detention before being proven guilty.
There are three types of bail in India123: Regular bail: This is granted to a person who has been arrested or is in police custody. A bail application can be filed for the regular bail under section 437 and 439 of Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC). Interim bail: This is granted as a temporary means and granted for a short period of time, either during the time of pendency of an application or when the application of anticipatory or regular bail is pending before the court. Anticipatory bail: This is granted to a person who apprehends arrest by the police for a non-bailable offence. A bail application can be filed for the anticipatory bail under section 438 of CrPC.
The grant of bail depends on various factors, such as the nature and gravity of the offence, the character and antecedents of the accused, the possibility of tampering with evidence or witnesses, etc. The court has the discretion to impose certain conditions on the bail, such as reporting to the police station, surrendering passport, etc.
